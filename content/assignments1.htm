Getting started with interviewing. Goal of this week is get familiar with interviewing.

  1. Conduct your interviews without any preparation.
  2. You may interview any person you like, but at least one you don't know.
  3. Approach the candidate with an opening sentence like: "Is it OK if I ask you some questions for an assignment of my course? It will take no more than 5 minutes. It is about the replacement of Blackboard. I've been asked to record our interview on video to get feedback on my interviewing technique. The video will be deleted after the course, and will not be distributed."
  4. Ask questions for your own sense making like: "Do you use Blackboard often?", “Do you like Blackboard?”, “What is good about it?“, “What do you not like about it?“
  5. Ask questions for data acquisition like: "What courses did you do last block before summer?" "How were they organized?" "Did you have to do labs?"
  6. Reflect with your partner.
  7. Log interesting findings and effective questions.

Ad 1 and 2 The first phase of any requirements is that of orientation: talking to people, making sense of what needs to be done. Given the amount of information available in even the smallest projects, it’s an illusion to study everything. The success of a requirements project is depending to quite some extent to the information we acquire and examine.

But how do we make the right selection? We like to see that we do this in a planned and controlled way, that we act as rational decision makers. Among others Klein, Weick and Kahneman showed that this is an illusion. We process information that is readily available. Even if for our problem it would have been better to consult other sources.

Learning to become a good requirements engineer is to learn to master your information acquisition process. You cannot fight nature: we have limited cognitive abilities. But we need to build complementary skills and repeatedly spent time to consciously consider if we are doing the right things.
